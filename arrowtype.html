Selecting the correct arrow type makes all the differance.

Compound

Most compound bows nowadays are adjustable. With just a tool, such as an Allen wrench, both the draw lenghth and pull weight can easily be attuned to your personal specification. There are a few ways to measure for draw length. The one I recommend is to measure from the tip of your middle finger to the other middle finger. Take that number and divide by 2.5. The quotient will be your draw length.

If the string does start to fray or even break, a compound bow press is required to string a new one. Hunters who unfortutionally face a damaged string, will find it challenging to replace on the grounds.

Crossbow

Crossbows can have a draw weight over 150 pounds. The bolts have the potentional of traveling at speeds of 180 fps or faster. Not many archers or people in general have the strength of loading 150lb string. Unlike other bows, the crossbow has a foot handle and a cocking rope that hooks on the string. An archer has only to place their foot on the handle and pull up on the rope until the string clicks in place. While this process greatly decreases the required strenghth, a fair amount of strength is still needed.
  • Bolts
  • Field Tips

Recurve Bow

Recurve bows were greatly used in the Dark Ages and can even be traced back to the earliest civilizations. These bows are the simplest to create and require little maintanance. If a frays or the bow itself gives way, construction a new one wouldn't take long. Ease of use as well as the plethora of new material, make the recurve a fan favorite. One downside of the recurve bow is the amount of time it requires to become proficient. Sure many newer recurve bows come ready to fit a sight, but that takes away the beauty of the age old art form.
